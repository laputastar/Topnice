IF WE DON'T STOP THEM THEY'LL KEEP COMING

Bed bugs are not a cleanliness problem. They are a logistics problem. They hitchhike. They hide. And by the time you know they're there, they've been there for weeks. By then they number in the tens to the hundreds, and they are in multiple rooms or apartments.

In 2021, I ran 2 hotels 20 miles apart.  One ran smoothly, and the other did not.  It was at the "did not" hotel that I was having bed bug problems.  I can remember my head of housekeeping coming to me and telling me that a housekeeper refused to clean a room because she found a bed bug in the room.  The head of housekeeping said she would not clean it either because she did not want to take bed bugs home with her, either.  I went to the room and saw my maintenance person dragging the linen to the bathtub so that he could heat treat the room to 139 degrees.

I was not happy.  First, he should not have been dragging the linen.  It should have been bagged up or left in the room to be heat treated.  Heat treating the area to 120 degrees for 4-hours kills everything.  I wanted them to heat treat for 24-hours at 139 degrees.  I wanted assured destruction of all stages of bed bug life.  Because of the way he was doing it, I couldn't be sure if the bed bug was there due to his failure [our failure] or was a new introduction.  In my frustration, I began thinking that there has to be a better way to find them before my staff and before my guest find them.  I wanted a way that detects them as soon as a guest brings them in.

This got me thinking about doorbell notices, and then drawing the bug in.  That is when I began formulating Pest Memo and getting a notification or alert that a bed bug was in the room.

Initially, I thought hotels would love this. With it you could tell a guest you proactively monitor for bed bugs and there hasn't been a bed bug in this room for 10 years.  As I spoke to different hotels, I was reminded that most hotels don't really have problems with bed bugs.  It is only once in a blue moon.  Then I started hearing about all of the problems with bed bugs in low-income housing. There was a tenant sitting across the street from his apartment building complaining about bed bugs in his apartment and no one caring.  My cousin lived in the same building, so I asked him.  He told me that he maintains his lease but does not sleep in the apartment because the bed bugs are so bad.

I spoke to others and I heard account after account about bed bugs going unremedied in low-income, multi-family housing. I even encountered a problem at my hotel from someone who lived in a low-income, high-rise.  She was a 90-year-old woman visiting her 90-year-old male friend.  Each time she came to visit we found a bed bug in his room despite our having moved his room to treat the last encounter.  I finally had to tell her she was not able to return to the hotel because of the bed bug problem.  Thankfully, she and I had a good rapport.  She accepted the news and understood.

I also found research generated by a HUD pest control consultant.  He noted: "In high-rise, senior living buildings, over 70% of residents are unaware they have bed bugs. • Shame and mental health issues are usually the problem."  Research he cited further noted that bed bugs show up in surrounding apartments within 3 days of entering the first unit.

In HUD-assisted multifamily housing, the problem is systemic. Traditional detection methods — physical inspections, canine units — cost hundreds of dollars per visit and only happen quarterly. In the interim, infestations spread unit to unit. A single missed detection in one apartment can trigger a building-wide outbreak within 30 days.
