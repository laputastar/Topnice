Risks and challenges
Every vinyl record is unique. Differences in age, manufacturing, storage conditions, material composition, and warp severity can all influence restoration results. While M2 is designed to safely reduce or eliminate many common warps, no record flattener can guarantee identical outcomes across every record.

Our philosophy has always been to prioritize record safety over aggressive restoration. Some severely warped records may require multiple cycles, while others may not be fully recoverable due to permanent deformation that occurred before treatment. Users should carefully read the manual and follow the recommended operating procedures for the best results.

From a production standpoint, M2 has already completed development and testing, with review units delivered to independent reviewers and production currently underway. While unforeseen manufacturing or logistics delays can occasionally occur, our vertically integrated manufacturing approach allows us to maintain close control over quality, production, and fulfillment.

We are committed to providing regular updates and maintaining open communication throughout the campaign and delivery process.

Why Warped Records Matter

A warped record doesn’t just create an audible wobble — it disrupts the entire playback system. Warps introduce irregular vertical and low-frequency motion that your stylus, cartridge, tonearm, and even speakers must react to. Over time, these unnatural movements can affect both sound quality and equipment longevity.

Stylus Tracking Distortion & Cartridge Wear

When a stylus encounters a warp, it must ride steep vertical waves instead of a stable groove. This can cause tracking distortion, pitch instability, increased surface noise, and in severe cases, mistracking or skipping.These repeated vertical impacts place additional stress on the cartridge suspension, cantilever, and stylus — accelerating wear beyond normal playback conditions.

Irregular Low-Frequency Motion Stresses Speakers

Warped records introduce subsonic low-frequency motion that may be inaudible but still forces your audio system to respond. This causes woofer cones to move excessively, even during quiet passages. Over time, this unnecessary excursion can strain speaker drivers, reduce dynamic range, and create visibly unstable playback as the system attempts to reproduce frequencies that were never part of the music.

Warps Reduce Grading & Collector Value

Warping is considered a structural defect in record grading. Even a playable record can be downgraded significantly, reducing resale value and making it less desirable to buyers. A record that would sell quickly when flat may sit unsold for weeks if visibly warped. Restoring a record helps preserve not only its playability, but also its collector value and market appeal.

Why Existing Solutions Fall Short

Warped records have been a challenge for collectors for decades, yet reliable and accessible solutions remain surprisingly limited. Many enthusiasts find themselves choosing between risky DIY methods and commercial machines that were never designed with the modern collector in mind.
